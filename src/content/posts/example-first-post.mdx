There is a moment in radiology that never becomes routine.

You pull up the images. The patient is somewhere down the hall — anxious, waiting, probably rehearsing worst-case scenarios in their mind. And you begin to look. Not at a person exactly, but through one. Slice by slice, the hidden becomes visible.

I have thought about this moment more than I should, probably.

---

## What the Body Hides

Medicine trains you to distrust surfaces. The patient who looks fine may be harboring something catastrophic. The scan that looks clean may have a finding you almost missed. Every clinician learns this the hard way, usually once, unforgettably.

But there is something theologically charged about the idea that **what is hidden is still real**. The mass does not become real when it is found. It was there before the scan, before the symptom, before anyone knew to look.

<div class="scripture">
  "You have searched me, Lord, and you know me. You know when I sit and when I rise; you perceive my thoughts from afar."
  <cite>— Psalm 139:1–2</cite>
</div>

The Psalmist is not reporting something uncomfortable. He is not confessing to surveillance anxiety. He is *marveling*. The God who sees through him — past the surface, past the performance, into the hidden — responds not with judgment but with presence.

---

## The Diagnosis That Changes Nothing and Everything

Here is the clinical parallel that I keep returning to: a diagnosis does not change what is there. It only changes what is known.

The patient with newly diagnosed hypertension did not acquire the disease at the moment of diagnosis. The atherosclerosis was building for years. The diagnosis simply marks the moment when the hidden crossed into the acknowledged.

God's omniscience works differently. He does not discover. He does not update His records. He simply *knows* — fully, simultaneously, without the lag that every human knower must accept.

What this means practically is startling: there is nothing you can reveal to God that He will be surprised by. The confession you have been rehearsing for years? He has been waiting for *you* to arrive at it, not to learn something new Himself.

---

## Why This Comforts Rather Than Terrifies

I have had patients who were afraid to get the scan. Not because they feared the procedure. Because they feared the knowing. As long as it was undiagnosed, a piece of them could still pretend.

I understand this. I have done the spiritual equivalent.

But the God of Psalm 139 does not use omniscience as a weapon. The Psalmist does not end the poem in a courtroom. He ends it with an invitation: *"Search me, God, and know my heart."*

The one who already knows everything is asked to look again — this time, with the patient's full consent.

That is not the posture of someone afraid of the scan. That is someone who has learned, slowly, that being known completely by a God who is love is not exposure. It is rest.

---

*These are the kinds of questions I sit with at the intersection of medicine and faith. If they resonate, I am glad for the company.*
