Greatness is having a moment.

It appears on hats and bumper stickers, in corporate retreat decks and Instagram bios, in the mouths of politicians and prosperity preachers. Everyone is selling greatness — or at least their version of it. Go higher. Rise faster. Do more. Be more. Get there before the other person does.

I want to sit with that for a moment before we open our Bibles. Because Jesus, in Mark 9, is doing something that cuts directly against every version of that story.

He is on the road to Capernaum. He has just come down from the Mount of Transfiguration, where three of His disciples watched His face become luminous and heard the voice of God declare: *"This is My beloved Son."* That is as high as a religious experience goes. And on the way down from that mountain, He gathers the twelve and tells them, with deliberate urgency, what is coming: *"The Son of Man is being betrayed into the hands of men, and they will kill Him. And after He is killed, He will rise the third day."*

Their response? They did not understand. And they were afraid to ask.

And then — within the same road, the same journey, before they had even reached the house — they began arguing among themselves about who was the greatest.

That juxtaposition is not accidental. It is the text. And it is the entry point into one of the most pastorally demanding passages in Mark's Gospel.

---

## The Fear That Looks Like Ignorance

The Greek word Mark uses in verse 32 is *ἀγνοέω* — *agnoeo* — translated here as "did not understand." It is the root of our English word *agnostic*. But in this context, it does not describe intellectual incapacity. The same word appears in Romans 10:3, where Paul describes those who, "being ignorant of God's righteousness," set about to establish their own. It is willful not-knowing. Strategic blindness.

Mark makes this explicit: they were afraid to ask.

I recognize this in clinical practice. I have watched patients sit through a consultation and not ask the question they came to ask — the one that had been turning over in their mind for weeks — because asking would make it real. The not-knowing is protective. If they do not ask, they do not have to hear the answer. If they do not hear the answer, they do not have to change their plans.

The disciples had plans. Positions. A kingdom that looked like every other kingdom — just with better theology layered on top. Jesus' passion prediction was a direct threat to that vision, so they filed it away. They protected themselves with confusion.

And then, having protected themselves from the hard truth, they filled the silence with something more comfortable: an argument about rank.

This is not ancient history. It is a description of every religious community that has ever avoided the costly implications of what Jesus actually said by staying very busy with questions about prestige and position. The fear underneath is always the same. The answer might cost us something.

## The Child in His Arms

When Jesus realized what they had been arguing about, He did not lecture them. He sat down — the formal posture of the rabbi delivering authoritative teaching — and He called the twelve. He said: *"If anyone desires to be first, he shall be last of all and servant of all."*

Then He reached for a child.

*Παιδίον* — *paidion* — a small child. In first-century Jewish and Roman society, a child had no legal standing, no social capital, no networking value, no ability to advance a career or protect a reputation. To serve a child was to do something that could never be repaid. The entire honor-shame architecture of the ancient world had no meaningful category for this kind of service. It was simply invisible.

Jesus took this invisible person, held them in His arms, and said: whoever welcomes one of these children in My name, welcomes Me. And whoever welcomes Me, welcomes the One who sent Me.

Let me stay here longer than feels comfortable.

*"Whoever receives one of these little children in My name receives Me; and whoever receives Me, receives not Me but Him who sent Me."* (Mark 9:37, NKJV)

That chain of identification — the powerless child equals Jesus equals the Father — is one of the most theologically dense statements in the Gospels. It is John 13 compressed into a single gesture with a small child. In that upper room, Jesus would kneel before His disciples with a basin and a towel and say: *"If I then, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also ought to wash one another's feet"* (John 13:14). The logic is identical. The one with all authority stoops to serve the one who could not reciprocate — and in that stooping, reveals the character of the kingdom. Here in Mark 9, He makes the same argument with a child instead of a basin.

It is also the entire kenosis of Philippians 2 made visible in a thirty-second object lesson. Paul would later describe what was happening in those moments of descent: *"He made Himself of no reputation, taking the form of a bondservant, and coming in the likeness of men"* (Philippians 2:7). Christ did not simply visit the low places — He emptied Himself to inhabit them. That same self-emptying logic is what Jesus is pressing into His disciples' hands along with the child. This is what love looks like when it has no audience and nothing to gain. The One who descended from the Father's glory is present in the person who has nothing to offer you. Not symbolically present. Actually present.

This is not a call to be kind to children. That would be easy. This is a call to a complete reorientation of the logic by which we determine whose welcome matters.

Who are the *paidion* in your congregation? Not children specifically — though perhaps them too. The person who comes once and never comes back. The elderly member who has no institutional influence. The newcomer who does not dress right or talk right. The person who is difficult to be around because they need more than they can give. The one who has nothing to offer the organization.

In Jesus' kingdom, they are not the least important people in the room. They are the measure of whether we have understood anything He has said.

They are the test.

## The Problem with Gatekeeping

John's intervention in verse 38 is, in its way, well-intentioned. He reports: *"Teacher, we saw someone who does not follow us casting out demons in Your name, and we forbade him, because he does not follow us."*

Read that sentence carefully. *We forbade him.* The imperfect tense in Greek — meaning they were actively, repeatedly stopping this man from doing what Jesus had never stopped doing.

And Mark, with his characteristic literary economy, places this immediately after Mark 9:18, where the disciples themselves had failed to cast out a demon. The disciples who could not heal were gatekeeping the man who could.

It would be easy to read this as a simple story about institutional pride — and it is that. But for those of us in the Seventh-day Adventist tradition, it creates productive tension that we need to sit with rather than quickly resolve.

We have a strong sense of prophetic identity. A remnant theology. A conviction that God has entrusted particular light to this movement for this hour. This is not, by itself, arrogance. There is biblical grounding for it. But John's error — confusing institutional loyalty with kingdom loyalty — is a temptation that becomes stronger, not weaker, when an institution has a high sense of its own theological precision.

Moses confronted the same dynamic in Numbers 11. Eldad and Medad were prophesying in the camp without authorization. Joshua said: *"Moses, my lord, forbid them!"* Moses replied: *"Are you zealous for my sake? Oh, that all the LORD's people were prophets."*

The irony bites: the disciples who argued about greatness were also the disciples who tried to control who else got to work in the kingdom. These are not separate problems. The desire to be first and the desire to control who belongs reinforce each other. If my position in this kingdom is defined by my proximity to the center, then anyone doing kingdom work from outside the authorized group is a structural threat.

Jesus is not calling us to doctrinal relativism. He is calling us to something harder: the recognition that God's Spirit is working in territory we have not mapped, through people whose ecclesiastical credentials we have not endorsed. Ellen White put it plainly: "We should not narrow our circle to include only those who believe just as we do" (*Testimonies for the Church*, Vol. 3, p. 435). The remnant call is a call to faithfulness. Not a license for exclusivity.

*He who is not against us is on our side.* That is not a small statement. It is a rebuke and an expansion at the same time.

## The Valley, the Worm, and the Mercy of Finality

The passage pivots in verse 42, and the language becomes severe.

*"Whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in Me to stumble, it would be better for him if a millstone were hung around his neck, and he were thrown into the sea."*

And then: the extended warnings about *Gehenna*. The hand, the foot, the eye — cut them off rather than be thrown into the fire "that shall never be quenched, where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched."

Christians have read this passage for centuries as a description of eternal conscious torment — souls burning forever in an unending fire. I want to argue, carefully and from the text itself, that it describes something quite different. Something that is, in fact, more consistent with the character of the God we encounter throughout this passage.

*Gehenna* — *Γέεννα* in Greek — is not a mythological underworld. It is a real place: the Valley of Hinnom, southwest of Jerusalem. Under Kings Ahaz and Manasseh, children were burned alive to the god Molech there (2 Chronicles 28:3; 33:6). Jeremiah prophesied that God would rename it "the Valley of Slaughter" (Jeremiah 7:31–32). By the Second Temple period, it had become Jerusalem's refuse dump — a place where fires burned continuously to consume waste. When first-century Jews heard *Gehenna*, they heard a geography of thorough, irreversible destruction.

When Jesus quotes the "worm that does not die and the fire that is not quenched," He is quoting Isaiah 66:24. Read it in context. It describes dead bodies outside the new Jerusalem being consumed by worm and fire. Not living souls in ongoing agony. Dead bodies. Being thoroughly, completely destroyed.

The fire is *unquenchable* — *πῦρ ἄσβεστον* — in the sense that nothing from outside can extinguish it before it has finished its work. Ezekiel 20:47 uses the same phrase for a literal forest fire that clearly ended. The fire accomplishes what it came to do, then it is done.

This matters enormously. The traditional doctrine of eternal conscious torment has done serious damage to our picture of God's character — the very thing this entire passage is about. A God who creates beings capable of infinite suffering and subjects them to it forever is not the God who holds a child in His arms and says: *this is what I look like*. The Adventist doctrine of conditional immortality — the "second death" of Revelation 20:14, which is final and irreversible annihilation, not infinite conscious torture — restores the moral coherence of divine justice.

God gives life. He can also withdraw it. The punishment is eternal in its *consequences*, not in its duration of conscious suffering.

The worm and the fire are not God's cruelty. They are His thoroughness.

## The Salt That Seasons Everything

The passage closes where it always needed to close: with salt.

*"For everyone will be seasoned with fire, and every sacrifice will be seasoned with salt. Salt is good, but if the salt loses its flavor, how will you season it? Have salt in yourselves, and have peace with one another."*

Salt is one of the most theologically loaded substances in the Old Testament. It sealed covenants (Leviticus 2:13; Numbers 18:19). It preserved without decay. A "covenant of salt" was the most binding kind of agreement — permanent, unfailing, the kind that did not spoil or become something other than what it was.

The fire that "salts" us in verse 49 is the fire of the Refiner — Malachi's image: *"He is like a refiner's fire... He will purify the sons of Levi and purge them as gold and silver."* This is not punitive fire. It is formative fire. It does not come to destroy the faithful. It comes to remove whatever in us would otherwise corrupt us.

Notice the grammar. In verse 49, *"everyone will be seasoned"* is passive voice — the seasoning is done *to* the disciple by an outside agent. Trials are not random. They are administered. There is a God who knows what we need and is actively working to produce it in us, even when — especially when — the process is costly.

And then: the passage that began with an argument about greatness closes with a command of *shalom*.

*"Have peace with one another."*

The Greek is *εἰρηνεύετε* — present imperative, an ongoing charge, not a one-time suggestion. From *eirēnē*, the Greek equivalent of the Hebrew *shalom*: wholeness, restored relationship, the harmony that exists where nothing is broken. Jesus does not resolve the greatness argument by declaring a winner. He transcends it. He points to the only salt that can season the whole community: peace that comes from people who have stopped competing and started serving.

The disciples who argued about greatness on the road to Capernaum argued about it again at the Last Supper (Luke 22:24). They never fully learned this lesson — not until Pentecost, when the fire that came upon them was the Spirit's fire, and the salt they received was the courage to pour their lives out for people who could not repay them.

That is the arc. That is what the fire was for all along.

## The Road He Walked First

There is a formation that happens in clinical training when you learn to move toward the difficult case rather than away from it — toward the patient who is frightened, toward the conversation that will not be easy, toward the truth that changes things. Medicine requires a practiced willingness to descend. The clinician who stays safely behind professional distance is not actually helping anyone. You have to go into the valley.

Jesus was on the road to Capernaum knowing the cross was at the end of it. He was not conserving emotional energy for the hard days ahead. He was not hurrying past the teaching moments to preserve Himself. He stopped. He sat down. He took a child in His arms. He answered John's gatekeeping with patience. He walked His disciples through the terrible geography of Gehenna and showed them what the fire was really for.

He was modeling the road as He walked it.

The road to true greatness runs through the valley. Through the child no one else is holding. Through the outsider doing kingdom work without your institution's endorsement. Through the trial you did not choose and cannot speed up. Through the relationship that requires the salt of genuine covenant love when you have very little left.

He walked this road first. That is not simply a comfort — it is a map.

Salt in yourselves. Peace with one another.

The road runs downward. That is how you know you are going the right way.
